\subsection{Motivation}
\label{subsec:motivation}

Classic slot car systems deliver an engaging tactile racing experience, but they remain bound to physical vehicles running on mechanical rails. In these systems, light and sound are treated as peripheral add-ons rather than as core gameplay elements: engine sounds are typically monotone and decoupled from the vehicle's actual speed, and illuminated track sections merely blink in fixed patterns instead of responding to the race state~\cite{carrera:2026light}. Even highly digitalized products such as Carrera Digital~132~\cite{carrera:2026digital}, Anki Overdrive~\cite{anki:2026overdrive} or Scalextric ARC~PRO~\cite{scalextric:2026arc} retain physical cars and use audiovisual feedback only as a secondary layer.

LED-based racing games take the opposite approach and represent each vehicle directly as a moving light on an individually addressable strip~\cite{openledrace:2023article,pereira:2023applying}. This removes all moving parts, yet existing projects stay deliberately minimalistic: gameplay is reduced to button mashing, and features such as a realistic physics model, spatially positioned audio, bidirectional lane changing, collision recovery, and modular track profiles are absent. As detailed in~\Cref{sec:related-work}, no existing system combines the physical realism and playability of slot cars with the mechanical simplicity and immersive, state-driven audiovisual feedback that addressable LEDs make possible. LumenTrace is motivated by closing precisely this gap.

\subsection{Contributions}
\label{subsec:contributions}

LumenTrace contributes a mechanics-free LED slot car simulator that unifies the strengths of both worlds on a distributed Raspberry~Pi Pico and Raspberry~Pi~4 architecture~\cite{magpi:2024raspberry}. 
At its core is a real-time physics engine that models acceleration, friction, speed clamping, collision detection, and crash recovery, driven by analog throttle and digital button inputs transmitted over a high-speed UART link~\cite{pena:2020uart}. On top of this, a priority-based LED rendering pipeline paints the track and vehicles with layered, state-dependent effects, including adaptive vehicle colors, headlights, and brake lights that give the driver ambient feedback about speed~\cite{meschtscherjakov:2015chaselight}, together with round counters realized through physically chained WS2812B matrices~\cite{garff:2023rpi}. 
A vectorized, thread-isolated stereo audio mixer~\cite{harris:2020array} adds per-vehicle engine sounds whose pitch and stereo position follow the car's speed and track position, complemented by positional event sounds. Playability is extended beyond prior LED racers through a temporary bidirectional overtaking lane and modular track modules with individual driving profiles. Finally, the system is packaged for reproducible deployment via Docker~\cite{merkel:2014docker} and ships with a hardwareless simulation interface for development and testing without physical hardware.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

LumenTrace sits at the intersection of two established domains: commercial slot car racing systems and open-source LED strip games. This section surveys both landscapes to contextualize the design decisions presented in the remainder of this report.

\subsection{Existing Slot Car Systems}
\label{subsec:existing-slot-car-systems}

Commercial systems have steadily added electronics while keeping physical cars on mechanical rails. Carrera Hybrid~\cite{carrera:2026hybrid} pushes toward a more digitalized and realistic racing simulation with collisions, overtaking, and modular track assembly. Carrera Digital~132~\cite{carrera:2026digital} offers button-triggered lane changes, lap counters, start lights, driver displays, and wireless controllers, but the vehicles remain fully physical rather than simulated. Scalextric ARC~PRO~\cite{scalextric:2026arc} adds an app-based interface with pit stops, lap-time tracking, weather conditions, and multiple race modes, while reducing the controller to a brake and a lane-change button. Anki Overdrive~\cite{anki:2026overdrive} abandons fixed slots in favor of app-controlled autonomous cars on magnetically connectable, modular track segments.

Across these systems, light and sound serve as secondary embellishments rather than integral gameplay elements. Many hobbyist Carrera GO!!!~\cite{carrera:2026carrera} layouts add track-side lighting such as traffic signals, small LCD readouts, or vehicle head- and tail-lights, yet these effects are neither integrated into the race flow nor used to illuminate the lanes themselves. The Carrera GO!!! Light and Sound Looping Set~\cite{carrera:2026light} is representative: roughly 16~LEDs on each side of the loop blink red and white as a car passes, accompanied by a fixed engine sound effect. Both the blinking and the sound are static and remain decoupled from the actual vehicle speed. Even in the most digital products, audio is dominated by the mechanical friction noise of the miniature cars and is limited to short indicator cues at the start and on completing a lap.

\subsection{LED-Based Projects and Racing Games}
\label{subsec:led-based-projects-and-racing-games}

A parallel line of work explores addressable LED strips as minimalist, one-dimensional game displays driven by simple controllers. Line Wobbler~\cite{baumgarten:2023line} is an award-winning one-dimensional dungeon crawler whose custom controller consists of a spring and a circa \SI{5}{\meter} LED strip; it has been widely exhibited in museums and has inspired follow-up projects such as the Arduino-based crawler TWANG~\cite{critters:2023twang}. These titles demonstrate the appeal of novel input mechanics and physical space, yet they remain single-player experiences without competitive racing elements. 

The most directly related project is the open-source Open LED Race (OLR) developed since 2019~\cite{pereira:2023applying}. OLR simulates a slot car track on a WS2812/WS2813 strip, representing each player as a colored light that advances by one acceleration pulse per button press, and supports up to four cars with optional slopes and gravity, a pit lane, and an electric-car mode requiring recharging~\cite{openledrace:2023article,openledrace:2025project}. The project has evolved through numerous community forks: extended gravity, sound, and lighting effects~\cite{arminjo:2025open}, a layout with 3D-printed track elements focussing on design~\cite{hackerspace:2024open}, an added 16\,$\times$\,16 LED matrix display~\cite{framboise:2024open}, a TinyGo reimplementation~\cite{esteban:2019ledrace}, and wireless ESP-based remote controllers~\cite{gmag11:2020ledrace}.

LumenTrace shares OLR's foundational ingredients of controller input, addressable LED strips, and microcontroller-based execution, but deliberately targets a different point in the design space. It omits OLR features such as gravity slopes, pit-lane recharging, and the electric-car mode, and instead introduces a realistic physics model, bidirectional lane changing, collision detection with crash recovery, priority-based immersive lighting, spatial audio, and modular track profiles. In doing so, LumenTrace bridges the realism and playability of commercial slot car systems with the flexibility and expressive audiovisual potential of LED-based games.

\section{System Requirements and Architecture}
\label{sec:system-requirements-and-architecture}

This section defines the functional and non-functional requirements that guided the development of LumenTrace and derives the system architecture that satisfies them.

\subsection{System Requirements}
\label{subsec:system-requirements}

The system requirements define both functional capabilities and non-functional performance constraints that ensure LumenTrace meets user expectations and technical standards.

\subsubsection*{Functional Requirements}

The initial project scope centered on developing a mechanics-free digital slot car racing system with a real-time control system, individually addressable RGB LED strips for track and vehicle visualization, basic sounds, and utilizing the Raspberry Pi Pico \SI{2}{\watt} microcontroller with PIO. Additionally, emphasis on design and playability was laid out to ensure an engaging user experience.

\subsubsection*{Non-Functional Requirements}

The system was designed to meet the following quality and performance attributes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Performance:}
    The system must render at greater than \SI{24}{\hertz} for human-perceivable smoothness. Controller inputs must be processed with minimal latency (less than \SI{100}{\milli\second} end-to-end) to provide responsive, immediate feedback during gameplay.
    
    \item \textit{Scalability:}
    The modular software architecture must support straightforward extension with new track modules, driving profiles, light and sound effects without requiring core system modifications.
    
    \item \textit{Safety:}
    All electrical hazards must be mitigated through proper insulation and power management. Mechanical components must be designed to eliminate sharp edges and pinch points accessible to users.
    
    \item \textit{Reliability and Availability:}
    The system must gracefully handle errors and automatically recover or restart. Cold boot initialization must complete within \SI{120}{\second} to minimize user wait time.
    
    \item \textit{Maintainability:}
    The codebase must be well-documented with clear separation of concerns. Hardware and software components must be independently testable and replaceable without system-wide redesign. A simulation interface must be provided for development and testing without requiring physical hardware.
    
    \item \textit{Usability:}
    The user interface must be intuitive and require minimal learning. Controller operation, track physics, and audiovisual feedback must be immediately comprehensible to new players. The game difficulty must be accessible to casual users while remaining engaging for experienced players.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{High-Level Architecture}
\label{subsec:high-level-architecture}

LumenTrace is built on a distributed two-tier architecture that splits the workload between an input-gathering microcontroller and a central core host~\cite{magpi:2024raspberry}, as depicted in~\Cref{fig:system-architecture}. The input controller captures the physical user commands and streams them over a serial connection to the main processing unit. The core host then executes the central application to resolve the game state and drive the output devices.

This separation of concerns offloads the latency-critical, high-frequency input sampling from the core host, allowing it to dedicate its computational resources to processing game state and real-time feedback without incurring polling overhead. Structuring the system into independent, well-defined subsystems also keeps it modular, maintainable, and scalable.

\section{Hardware Implementation}\label{sec:hardware-implementation}

This section describes the hardware realization of LumenTrace, covering the distinct roles of the two microcontrollers, the serial communication interface between them, and the physical construction of the track and controllers.

\subsection{Microcontroller Setup}
\label{subsec:microcontroller-setup}
% - Raspberry Pi Pico as input MCU: MicroPython firmware, permanent and latency-free reading of analog controllers and digital buttons.
% - Raspberry Pi 4 as core host: main application, game logic, physics ticks, LED display buffers, threaded audio mixer.
% -> Hardware diagram

As illustrated in~\Cref{fig:hardware-setup}, the specific hardware configuration establishes the low-level execution environments for both devices. The Raspberry~Pi Pico acts as the dedicated input microcontroller running MicroPython firmware~\cite{george:2026micropython}. It permanently and latency-free samples the physical controllers, utilizing its internal 12-bit analog-to-digital converters (ADCs) channels to read the analog throttles and its GPIO pins to detect digital button presses.

Conversely, the Raspberry~Pi~4 functions as the core host, booting its operating system and the main Python application from a micro-SD card to enable fully autonomous physical startup. The Pi~4 processes the inputs transmitted by the Pico to maintain the internal game state and execute physics ticks. It translates this game state into audiovisual feedback by actuating the LED strips and matrices~\cite{garff:2023rpi}, and by driving the stereo audio mixer~\cite{geier:2024sounddevice,bechtold:2026soundfile}.

\subsection{Communication Interface}
\label{subsec:communication-interface}

The Pico and the Pi~4 communicate over a high-speed UART link~\cite{pena:2020uart,liechti:2020pyserial}. On every polling cycle, the Pico serializes the current state of all connected controllers into a compact JSON structure~\cite{json:2026introducing} and transmits it to the Pi~4. Delegating input acquisition and serialization to the Pico relieves the core host from polling overhead, so that it can consume the most recent controller state directly instead of sampling the hardware itself. As shown in~\Cref{fig:controller-json}, each controller entry reports a unique \texttt{controller\_id}, the raw analog throttle value \texttt{adc\_0}, and the digital button state \texttt{dig\_0}.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{../diagrams/out/json-uart-controller-signal/json-uart-controller-signal.png}
    \caption{Example of the JSON structure transmitted from the Pico to the Pi~4 over UART, containing the current state of two controllers.}
    \label{fig:controller-json}
\end{figure}

At an update rate of \SI{50}{\hertz}, the protocol tolerates sporadic transmission losses: roughly every twentieth packet is dropped or unreadable, yet a single missing update is uncritical because the game engine simply continues with the last known controller state until the next valid packet arrives.

\subsection{Physical Design and Controllers}
\label{subsec:physical-design-and-controllers}

The physical setup is built on a black wooden base plate of \SI{150}{\centi\meter}~$\times$~\SI{80}{\centi\meter}, spray-painted in blue, purple, and white to create a retro-futuristic backdrop. 
The track layout reuses Carrera~GO!!!~\cite{carrera:2026carrera} track modules, along with two \SI{5}{\meter} highly diffusing LED strips carrying \num{50} LEDs per meter (\num{250} LEDs in total), to visualize the lanes, vehicles and intersections. All components were rigidly fastened using screws, cyanoacrylate adhesive, and double-sided tape to preserve transportability, and the loop module was additionally reinforced with fishing line to prevent fractures.

The original Carrera~GO!!! hand controllers were modified from their built-in variable resistors into signal sources for the Pico. Each throttle is wired as a voltage divider feeding one of the Pico's ADC channels, yielding the raw analog value transmitted as \texttt{adc\_0}, while an added push button provides the digital lane-change signal (\texttt{dig\_0}). 

Custom 3D-printed enclosures, styled to match the sci-fi aesthetic, conceal the Raspberry~Pi~4, the Pico, the internal wiring, and house the stereo speakers. Beyond their visual role, these enclosures also serve a safety function: together with the only \SI{3}{\ampere} power draw per LED strip, they cover the electronics to prevent user contact and eliminate sharp edges and pinch points.

\section{Project Management}
\label{sec:project-management}

The LumenTrace project was executed by a three-person team, with each member contributing to different aspects of the project. 
The lead developers for each component are indicated in~\Cref{tab:task_distribution}, while a reviewer was assigned to each task to ensure hardware and software quality. 

The development of LumenTrace followed an incremental path, prioritizing a basic architecture and core functionality before implementing additional features and working on enhancements. 
The project was structured into four distinct milestones to manage dependencies and verify integration at each phase.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Milestone 1: Proof of Concept (POC):}
          Establishment of the physical Carrera GO!!!~\cite{carrera:2026carrera} track, development of Raspberry Pi Pico firmware using MicroPython, implementation of analog controller input reading, establishment of UART serial communication, and design of the JSON-based data serialization and parsing.

    \item \textit{Milestone 2: Base Game Implementation:}
          Software architecture with modular packages, core physics engine with acceleration, friction, and speed clamping, vehicle lane management and position tracking, collision detection via distance metrics, multithreaded game loop architecture, LED strip control, display rendering pipeline with priority-based layer system, modular track design with individual driving profiles, settings system, unit testing framework, and centralized logging via file and MQTT interfaces.

    \item \textit{Milestone 3: Enhancements and Feature Expansion:}
          Advanced physics for lane changes, respawn system, intersection modules, virtual LED strip abstraction for hardware constraint resolution, round counters, vehicle lighting effects including headlights, rear brake lights, and identification lights, real-time vectorized stereo audio mixer with volume and stereo panning controls, threaded sound manager, per-vehicle engine sounds with speed-responsive pitch, event-driven sound effects, Docker containerization, 3D-printed controller enclosures and component styling, and performance optimization to prevent thread starvation.

    \item \textit{Milestone 4: Documentation and Finalization:}
          Comprehensive technical report with architecture diagrams and system analysis, usage guides, technical report documentation, and project preparation for submission.
\end{itemize}

The division of responsibilities between the three team members is outlined in~\Cref{tab:task_distribution}. In general, Simon Völkl focused on the software implementation, Erik Dombrowsky concentrated on the hardware wiring and physical setup, while Simon Frey contributed mainly to the 3D printing and styling of the components.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

\subsection{Performance Analysis}
\label{subsec:performance-analysis}

The LumenTrace system achieves real-time performance across all critical subsystems, meeting or exceeding the specified non-functional requirements.

The system operates three independent processing threads at optimized frequencies: \SI{50}{\hertz} for the game engine physics, \SI{33}{\hertz} for the LED display pipeline, and \SI{50}{\hertz} for controller input polling. These frequencies produce smooth, flicker-free visual output perceived as continuous motion above the human perception threshold of circa \SI{24}{\hertz}~\cite{chen:2007review,pazhoohi:2021effect}.

Controller inputs demonstrate minimal end-to-end latency from physical button press through UART transmission, game logic processing, and LED display update. The system responds immediately to throttle and lane-change commands with no perceptible delay. This responsiveness is maintained even during occasional UART transmission failures (occurring in approximately \SI{5}{\percent} of packets), as the system prioritizes the most recent controller state rather than queuing stale data.

The optional file and MQTT logging subsystems introduce measurable performance overhead due to I/O blocking and possible network latencies. Logging should remain disabled in production environments, else performance degradation and occasional \SI{50}{\milli\second}--\SI{200}{\milli\second} lag spikes during log message transmission can occur. Deferred asynchronous logging could mitigate this overhead in future iterations.

\subsection{User Experience and Feedback}
\label{subsec:user-experience-and-feedback}

User testing and informal feedback sessions revealed that the combination of immersive LED lighting effects, spatially-positioned audio, and responsive physics created a compelling and engaging experience. The retro-futuristic aesthetic, with its purple and blue color scheme and sci-fi-styled 3D-printed components, resonated well with both casual observers and competitive players. Iterative refinements to speed limit transitions and audio volume levels improved overall player satisfaction and reduced jarring gameplay moments. Although the system currently relies on Carrera GO!!!~\cite{carrera:2026carrera} track modules, custom modules are planned for future development to enhance flexibility and independence from proprietary track systems. For museum exhibitions or trade show presentations, further concealment of cables is recommended to ensure safety and prevent potential malfunctions like loose connections.
